Mostly water

You are a body
of water

Roughly sixty percent of you is water. Your blood runs close to the salt of an old sea; each of your cells is a droplet that learned to hold a shape. Before it was ever "you," this water was rain, river, cloud, glacier, and the warm inside of other creatures.

Astrida Neimanis puts it plainly: we are, each of us, a body of water — not a sealed vessel with water stored inside, but water itself, gathered for a while into the shape we answer to.

And it doesn't stop at your skin. You drink the sky and breathe out the sea; you pass your water on. You are less a thing than a place — somewhere the planet's water is briefly passing through.

You are not a container of water. You are water, briefly holding a shape.

Transcorporeality

Nothing here
is only yours

Every one of us began inside another body of water. Neimanis calls this gestationality: your first world was someone else's inner sea. We are each born of a body of water — and built from water that others, human and not, once carried.

Stacy Alaimo's word is transcorporeality — matter moving across the line we like to draw around a self. What is "environment" one minute is "you" the next: the river becomes your blood, your breath becomes the weather, the fish carry traces of us and we of them.

So there is no clean edge. We are all downstream of one another — of every creature and cloud that held this water first, and every one who'll hold it next. The membrane is real, but it leaks. Belonging isn't a bond we add on afterward; it's the water already moving through.

We are all downstream of one another.
Bodies of Water · Stimpunks 3
Differentiation

The confluence keeps
the difference

Here's the trap to refuse. If we all flow together, doesn't difference dissolve — everyone melting into one? Neimanis is careful: watery belonging is not a blur into sameness. Water is the great archive. It carries what passed through it; it stays particular; it remembers.

You can watch this where two rivers meet. A confluence runs braided for miles downstream — one channel, two waters, still visibly distinct. Joined and different at the same time.

That is the neurodiversity paradigm in a single image. Belonging as flow doesn't sand us down to a common shape. The spiky profile survives the merge. We are in it together and each unmistakably ourselves — no baseline to dissolve toward, no norm the water is trying to reach.

Belonging doesn't ask you to lose your shape.

joined and different at once
interbeing without erasure

Collective flow

Where the
currents pool

Attention flows too. Monotropism (Murray, Lesser & Lawson) describes minds that pour attention into a few deep channels at a time — a strong current, not a thin spread. In flow, the channel becomes the whole world.

And currents can meet. When our attention tunnels line up — same interest, same rhythm, working side by side — they join into collective flow: parallel play, shared hyperfocus, the easy company of people deep in the same thing. Autistic Realms (Helen Edgar) names it shared flow — attention turned toward one another's, holding a channel open together.

A watering hole is where flows gather — the literal one, where creatures meet to drink, and the kind Stimpunks builds into Cavendish Space: a place made for attention to pool without anyone being pushed out of their own channel. Belonging, here, is a confluence of attention.

Flow isn't only solitary. Currents find each other.
Bodies of Water · Stimpunks 5
Belonging as flow

Build the
watering hole

If belonging is a flow, the work isn't to make people fit. It's to keep the water moving. You don't answer a drought by lecturing the fish about resilience — you restore the flow.

So we build watering holes: places where currents of attention and care can gather; where you can drink without being drained; where no one has to leave their channel to be let in. More-than-human kin included — every body that shares this water has standing at the pool.

Belonging isn't earned by sameness or paid for in masking. It runs upstream and downstream of you already. The task was never to prove you deserve the water. The task is to unblock it.

Notdissolution — belonging never asks you to lose your shape.
Notthe sealed self — no body of water is only itself.
Notpurity — "clean vs. contaminated" is the old language of eugenics; water mixes, and so do we.
Notflow-as-productivity — we reclaim flow as joy and company, not output.

keep the water moving
restore the flow

The oldest water

We are all
bodies of water

Follow the water back far enough and it leaves the planet. Much of Earth's water is older than the Sun — formed in cold interstellar clouds and carried in on ice and comets before there was an ocean to hold it. The sea in your cells is partly starlight's leftover frost.

It has been vapour, rain, river, glacier, whale, fern, and stranger — and it will be again, long after us. You are one shape it is taking, on loan, for a little while.

So when you feel the pull toward others — toward shared water, shared attention, shared air — that isn't neediness to apologise for. It's the truest thing about you. We are all bodies of water, all made of star stuff, all borrowing the same old sea. Love you down to your star stuff.

We are all bodies of water. You were never a closed thing.

Reflection

Whose water are you made of — who and what held it before it was you?

Where have you found collective flow — attention shared, side by side, without being pushed out of your own channel?

What blocks the flow around you, and what would it take to unblock it?

Bodies of Water is the twenty-third zine in the Stimpunks series. It reads belonging as flow, and joins the water kin — The Nearest Body (No. 17) and The Same Water (No. 20).

The thinking is Astrida Neimanis's: Hydrofeminism: Or, On Becoming a Body of Water, and Bodies of Water: Posthuman Feminist Phenomenology — gestationality, the hydrocommons, and belonging held together with difference; with Stacy Alaimo on transcorporeality. The science: the adult body is roughly 60% water; the global water cycle; and the evidence that much of Earth's water predates the Sun, carried in from cold interstellar clouds. The collective-flow thread is ours and our kin's: monotropism (Murray, Lesser & Lawson); Stimpunks' Flow; and shared / collective flow from Autistic Realms (Helen Edgar) — Monotropism and Collective Flow and Shared Flow — with More Realms on monotropic flow as creative practice.

The star stuff is Carl Sagan's, and the SN 1006 remnant on every Stimpunks page. The reading — that belonging is a current, not a border — is ours. You were ★stuff, borrowing the same old sea, all along.
