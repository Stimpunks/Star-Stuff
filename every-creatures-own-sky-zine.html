Umwelt · the sealed world

No view from
nowhere

A century ago, the biologist Jakob von Uexküll asked a strange question: what is the world like for a tick? The answer is almost nothing — and yet complete. She waits, blind and deaf, for three signals: the warmth of a mammal, the smell of butyric acid, the touch of skin. On those three her whole life turns. That is her Umwelt — not a poorer version of our world, but her entire sky.

This is the move that changes everything. Each creature lives within its own perceptual world, and each world is whole — not a partial glimpse of one true reality waiting underneath. There is no underneath. There is no view from nowhere. But whole is not the same as walled: a sky is a soap bubble, not a fortress — permeable, kept aloft by encounter, and never guaranteed.

The "real world" — neutral, default, the one we assume we all share — no one has ever sensed it. We have only ever had windows.

each world entire, none the whole
no default underneath

The senses beyond ours

A sky you
can't imagine

In 1974, the philosopher Thomas Nagel asked what it is like to be a bat — and answered, honestly, that we cannot know. A bat paints the dark with echoes; its world is sound given shape. You can describe it from outside forever and still never climb in.

The parade is long. The mantis shrimp reads light through a dozen-plus photoreceptor types — not more of our colour, but a wholly different instrument for parsing it. The dog lives in scent-time, where a breeze says who passed and how long ago; smell is its history and its news. The songbird feels magnetic north; the star-nosed mole sees with touch.

None of these is the correct one. Each is a sky, entire.

Every Creature's Own Sky · Stimpunks 3
The cosmos looks back

Star stuff,
sensing

Here is the Star Stuff turn. The same exploded stars that became the tick and the bat and you also became the only eyes the universe will ever have. There is no cosmic observer floating above it all, seeing things as they "really" are. The cosmos sees itself only from the inside — through compound eyes and echoes, through scent and skin.

So perception was plural from the very first light. Not one true picture with a lot of flawed copies, but millions of windows, each opening onto something real, none of them the whole. The universe senses itself in dialect.

A single sky would have been a smaller universe. This one chose the fan of them.

There is no view from above the cosmos. It sees itself only from the inside — a million windows, none of them the whole.

the cosmos sees from inside
sensing itself in dialect

Attention-worlds

Minds have
skies too

If no two bodies share a sky, why would two minds? Attention is a sense too — it decides what shows up at all. Some minds spread attention wide and thin across a whole scene; others pour it into a few deep channels, and the world arranges itself around those. This is monotropism: an attention-world with tall, narrow windows and very bright light inside them.

Ours is not a broken copy of the standard mind. There is no standard mind, any more than there is a standard sky. What looks from outside like missing the picture is, from inside, a different and complete way of being here — autistic perception, whole on its own terms.

The tick is not a failed dog. We are not failed anyone.

There is no standard mind, any more than there is a standard sky.
Every Creature's Own Sky · Stimpunks 5
The double empathy problem

The gap goes
both ways

Now the quiet payoff. If a human and a bat could sit down to talk, the failure to understand would run both directions — not the bat falling short of the human. When two umwelten meet, the gap is mutual, and it belongs to neither of them.

Damian Milton named this the double empathy problem: when an autistic and a non-autistic person misread each other, it isn't one broken decoder and one working one. It's two skies, two dialects, meeting at a border. The deficit was never inside a person. It lives in the space between worlds — and a space belongs to everyone standing in it.

And the border is not level ground: when a world insists on one right way to sense, it is always the same sky asked to flex or fall silent. Here, translation is survival, not courtesy — the soap bubble under constant pressure is the one that pops.

Nota deficit in one party — the gap is mutual, and it belongs to no one alone.
Notone correct channel — there is no default sky to fall short of.
Not"you decode us while we stay unread" — translation runs both ways.
Notempathy as making you legible — curiosity, not correction.

two worlds meeting at a border
mutual, not one-sided

translation is two-way labour
no one owes you legibility

What the sky asks of us

Mutual
translation

So what do you do, standing at the border of another sky? Not correct it toward yours. You learn its dialect — the way Vinciane Despret says we should study a creature by asking the questions that matter to it, not to us.

No one owes you legibility. The bat need not echo in words; we need not smooth ourselves into the neurotypical. Translation is two-way labour, and it begins by granting that the other world is real, and whole, and not yours.

We are the cosmos doing this to itself — one window turning, with effort and delight, to learn what another window sees. Empathy is the universe translating between its own skies.

We are the cosmos learning to translate between its own windows — real, and whole, and not each other's.

Reflection

Whose way of sensing have you been treating as the default — the neutral, "real" one?

Where have you called a difference a deficit, when it was really a different sky?

At a border with another mind or creature, who gets expected to do the translating?

Every Creature's Own Sky is the fifteenth zine in the Stimpunks series. It carries the Umwelt thread from No. 14, The Council of All Beings inward — from how we govern other worlds to how we sense at all.

The ideas are real and worth naming precisely. Umwelt — each creature's self-enclosed perceptual world, and the tick with her three signals — is Jakob von Uexküll's, from A Foray into the Worlds of Animals and Humans. "What is it like to be a bat?" is Thomas Nagel (1974). The mantis shrimp's dozen-plus photoreceptor types appear to support a fundamentally different colour system rather than finer discrimination (Thoen et al., 2014) — a different instrument, not simply "more colour." Dogs living in scent-time draws on Alexandra Horowitz, Inside of a Dog.

Minds have skies too: monotropism is from Dinah Murray, Mike Lesser & Wenn Lawson (2005), developed since by Fergus Murray and the monotropism community; "autistic perception" is Erin Manning's. The bridge from animal Umwelten to autistic being-in-the-world runs through Ombre Tarragnat's work on ethodiversity and ethodivergence (incl. "A Foray into the Bubbles of Autistics" and "The Ethodivergent Manifesto"); the reframing of the umwelt as a permeable, precarious soap bubble — whole but never guaranteed — is hers too, after Neimanis. The double empathy problem is Damian Milton (2012): mutual, not one-sided. Asking the questions that matter to the creature is Vinciane Despret.

The reading — that there is no neutral sky, that minds are umwelten too, and that empathy is the cosmos translating between its own windows — is ours. Every window is star stuff, looking.
