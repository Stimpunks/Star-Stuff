Niche construction

Organisms are
not passive

Since Darwin, we have pictured the environment as the fixed thing: it sets the test, and organisms adapt or die. But that is only half the loop. The beaver builds a dam and turns a stream into a pond. The spider spins a web; the bird weaves a nest; the earthworm rewrites the chemistry of the soil. Organisms are not passive. The environment is a product of organisms.

Biologists call this niche construction (Odling-Smee, Laland & Feldman), and it runs as a second engine of evolution beside natural selection. It even has its own inheritance: descendants receive not only genes but the world their ancestors built — a beaver born into the pond its grandparents dug.

So life does not only drift and sense. It builds — and the built world is handed on.

Evolution has two engines: the genes you inherit, and the world your ancestors built.

back from the Council, and building
the world remade, and inherited

Positive niche construction

Don't fix the head,
change the world

Here is what that means for us. The old model says that when a mind doesn't fit, you fix the mind — make it more like the standard one. Niche construction says the standard was never sacred. As Thomas Armstrong argues, there is no "normal" brain kept in a vat at the Smithsonian for all other brains to be measured against. So stop bending the person. Build the environment.

This is not soft metaphor; it is how minds actually work. We ease a too-loud world by acting on it — dimming the light, finding the quiet room, laying the routine down like track. Cognitive science frames it as acting on the world to suit our own states; the plainer version is that the place itself helps us think. Cognition is spread across the room we arrange, not sealed in the skull.

Change the world, and you change what a mind can do.

Don't bend the mind to fit the room. Build a room the mind can think in.
Minor Worlds · Stimpunks 3
A space of one's own

Stimming is
worldmaking

Watch that building up close. The noise-cancelling headphones. The same walk, the same meal, the song on repeat. The desk set just so; the shelf that holds the whole special interest; the stone turned over and over in a hand. From outside, these get filed as symptoms to manage. From inside, they are worldmaking — the patient assembly of a few stable points a self can stand on.

Stimming is niche construction at the scale of a body: movement that tunes the sensory field until it is livable. Ombre Tarragnat calls such movements ontological choreographies — dances that bring a small world into being. One autistic writer describes their nest plainly: this is my space; it lets me hold one small part of a hard world.

Own Sky left us inside a soap bubble — whole, but never guaranteed. Here is the reply to that precarity: we build the bubble ourselves, and we keep on rebuilding it.

a world assembled from chosen things
we build the bubble ourselves

Niche communities

Minor worlds

These worlds are small, and the smallness is the point. A corner, a routine, a fandom, a friendship, a special interest — none of them universal, every one of them real. Erin Manning calls this the minor: not lesser, only not pretending to be the whole.

And we build them together. Autistic people all but invented fandom; we assemble niche communities out of necessity and because our ways of being are, frankly, weird (Devon Price). Some grow into NeurodiVentures — non-hierarchical groups run by neurodivergent people for collaborative niche construction, thickening over time into resilient little ecosystems.

Small, local, tended by the people who need it — that is not a lesser world. That is a world.

A world doesn't have to be universal to be real. It only has to be built, and shared.
Minor Worlds · Stimpunks 5
Reconstruction, not accommodation

Whose niche is
the default?

But not every niche admits that it was built. The hum of fluorescent light, the open-plan clatter, the crowded room — these get called neutral, just the conditions everyone works in. They are nothing of the sort. They are a niche too, built to suit one kind of mind and then renamed the default.

Kadodia and Krueger call it the epistemic niche: our shared spaces quietly encode whose senses, whose pace, whose ways of knowing count, and reward the people they were built for. Which is why bolting an accommodation onto a hostile room is not the fix. The architecture itself has to change — reconstruction, not accommodation.

There is no neutral sky and no neutral room. And as biodiversity toughens an ecosystem, a wider range of minds toughens a society's knowledge: the default was only ever one niche among the many we could build.

Notan accommodation bolted onto a hostile room — reconstruction, not a ramp to nowhere.
Nota "neutral" default — a default is just someone's niche, left unmarked.
Notbending the person to the room — build a room more minds can think in.
Nota world you're only allowed to visit — a world you get to help build.

peel back "neutral"
and find a niche, unmarked

Minor Worlds · Stimpunks 6

a niche is always made with
we world each other into being

The oldest work

Worlding
together

The last thing to notice is that we never build alone. A niche is made with — with the people who share the fandom, the dog who sets the shape of the day, the plants on the sill, the tools on the belt. Donna Haraway calls these companion species: we world one another into being.

Step back far enough and it is all one story. The beaver's pond, the spider's web, the quiet room, the niche community, the reconstructed school — every one of them is matter arranging itself into a place where life can hold. The universe builds itself into worlds, and it does it through us.

We are star stuff, building — and to make one small, livable world, together, is the oldest work there is.

We are star stuff, building. To make a world is the oldest thing matter does.

Reflection

What have you had to build for yourself to make a hard world livable?

Which "neutral" rooms in your life are really built for one kind of mind?

Whose niche gets called the default — and who has to keep visiting instead of building?

Minor Worlds is the sixteenth zine in the Stimpunks series, and the third of a loose trilogy: how we treat other worlds (No. 14), how we sense them (No. 15), and — here — how we build them. It is the one where the cosmology comes home to the ground the Foundation stands on.

Niche construction — organisms remaking their environments, and passing the remade world on as ecological inheritance — is from Odling-Smee, Laland & Feldman (2003), after Lewontin; the beaver, spider, and nest are the textbook cases. Positive niche construction — change the environment, not the child; there is no "normal" brain in a vat — is Thomas Armstrong's (Neurodiversity in the Classroom). That we act on the world to suit our states, and that "the place itself helps us think," draw on active-inference research on autistic cognition and Annie Murphy Paul's The Extended Mind.

Stimming as worldmaking, and the precarious yet buildable bubble, draw on Ombre Tarragnat (autistic worldings, ontological choreography) and Erin Manning (the minor, autistic perception); the nest that holds "one small part of a hard world" is from Emergent Divergence. Niche communities and fandom are Devon Price (Unmasking Autism) and Steve Silberman (NeuroTribes); NeurodiVentures and collaborative niche construction are Jorn Bettin / Autistic Collaboration. The epistemic niche — "neutral" defaults built for one kind of mind, reconstruction over accommodation — is Kadodia & Krueger (2026). Companion species is Donna Haraway; "matter worlds" is Karen Barad. Monotropism carries over from No. 15.

The reading — that no niche is neutral, no world is lesser, and that building them is the cosmos's oldest work — is ours. You were star stuff, building, all along.
