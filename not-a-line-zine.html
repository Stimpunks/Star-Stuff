Spectroscopy

A spectrum is
not a line

Split white light through a prism and you don't get a ranking from a bad end to a good end. You get a fan of every wavelength at once — red through violet, none higher or lower, just light spread out by colour.

Now split the light of a star. Threaded through its spectrum are fine dark and bright lines — and here's the wonder: every element leaves its own exact pattern of them, a fingerprint. Hydrogen signs one way, helium another, iron another still. Reading those lines is the only way we know what the stars are made of.

So a spectrum was never a scale of more-or-less. It is a signature of what something is made of.

A spectrum doesn't rank a star. It tells you what the star is made of.

a fan, then a signature
this is how we read the stars

The severity scale

But we heard
a line

Then the word crossed into talk about autism, and something was lost in translation. Spectrum got heard as a line: mild at one end, severe at the other, everyone slotted somewhere between. High-functioning here, low-functioning there. "A little bit autistic." "We're all somewhere on it."

Patrick Dwyer names the problem plainly: a single line from "highest" to "lowest functioning" erases how the same person struggles more or less in different domains, on different days, in different rooms. And functioning labels don't just mislead — they gatekeep, sorting us into "not disabled enough" to deserve support or "too disabled" to be trusted with our own choices.

The word meant fingerprint. We heard ladder.

"Mild to severe" measures how much you inconvenience us — not who you are.
Not a Line · Stimpunks 3
The spiky profile

Better — but
still a grid

Many of us met a better picture: the spiky profile. Not uniformly mild or severe but jagged — high in some domains, low in others, peaks and troughs across social, sensory, cognitive, and motor. It kills the single dial, and that matters.

But as Helen Edgar points out, the spiky profile still plots us on a grid. It reads our peaks and dips as distances from a flat, expected baseline — the line of neuronormativity — frozen at one moment of assessment. A better chart is still a chart of how far we fall from normal.

It traded one line for a row of them. Progress — but still a grid, and still a baseline we're measured against.

The spiky profile stopped ranking us on one line — and started ranking us on many.

jagged, but still plotted
a better chart is still a chart

a figure, not a score
read from where you stand

The constellation

A constellation,
not a chart

So drop the grid entirely. Patrick Dwyer offers a different figure: an autism constellation — not a point on a scale but many stars in many positions, each autistic person a different arrangement of them.

Helen Edgar carries it further: a constellation of intensities. Not points plotted on a chart but an interdependent, monotropic galaxy — sensory, cognitive, and relational ways of being in ever-shifting relation. A constellation has no baseline and no rank; its stars sit at wildly different distances and cohere into a figure only from where you happen to stand. Relational, contextual, alive.

That is us: not a measurement, but a pattern of light.

always in motion, always connecting, always becoming — never fixed, never finished.— Helen Edgar
Not a Line · Stimpunks 5

free of the ruler
no norm star

Difference first

No line, no centre,
no norm

Here is what a constellation quietly refuses. With no line, there's no top to reach and no bottom to fall to — and no centre either. The whole machinery of functioning levels assumed a norm to measure our distance from. A constellation has no norm star.

This is the frame we keep returning to: difference comes first. Difference isn't deviation from a standard human; the standard was a story we told — and then used to decide who got believed, supported, or dismissed.

And — this matters — refusing the line is not refusing that some of us need far more support. It refuses that support needs rank us. The honest replacement for a label was never a better label; it's naming the specific support each of us actually needs. No line. And still, all the care.

Nothigh- or low-functioning — labels that gatekeep support, not descriptions of us.
Notmild or severe — a fingerprint has no volume knob.
Not"a little bit on the spectrum" — the spectrum was never a little-to-a-lot.
Nota dial to be set — a constellation to be read.

the two skies, at once
signature and figure

The whole light

Every star its
own spectrum

Bring the two skies together. Read any star's light and you get not a rank but a fingerprint — this one rich in iron, that one in helium, each unmistakably itself. And no star sits on a line; they hang at every distance, making figures only a living eye can join.

We are both at once: each of us a whole spectrum, a signature no one else carries — and together a constellation, a pattern that shifts with where we stand and who we stand among. The rainbow on every page of this site was never a ladder from red to violet. It's the whole light, arriving at once.

This is what the creed meant all along: every colour, key, and frequency of neurodiversity. You are not high or low on anything. You are star stuff, reading itself.

You are not a point on a line. You are a whole spectrum — a constellation no one has ever seen before.

Reflection

Where have you been handed a functioning label — and what did it gatekeep?

If you drew yourself as a constellation instead of a score, what would be in it?

What specific support do you actually need — named, not ranked?

Not a Line is the twenty-first zine in the Stimpunks series, and it argues for the metaphor Star Stuff already runs on: the sky is organised by constellations, not lines. It is built, with gratitude, on two pieces — Patrick Dwyer's "The Autism Constellation" (Autistic Scholar, 2020) and Helen Edgar's "The Constellation of Intensities: Neuroqueering the Spiky Profile" (Autistic Realms, 2026), whose line → spiky profile → constellation is this zine's spine.

The metaphor is older than any one essay, and belongs to the autistic community. Dwyer notes in a 2021 update that he did not coin it: Caroline Hearst had built a physical autism-constellation model and shown it at events, and the idea was already moving by word of mouth before that — with Larry Arnold an early proponent and Damian Milton and the late Dinah Murray discussing similar concepts. In correspondence with Helen Edgar, Milton credits Larry Arnold as the first he knows of to use "constellation" in place of "spectrum." We name them here because a zine against ranking people should not erase the people whose idea this is.

The science: dispersion and the prism (Newton); the electromagnetic spectrum; spectroscopy and spectral lines (Fraunhofer; Kirchhoff & Bunsen), by which each element's fingerprint reveals what stars are made of; and the constellation as a relational, perspective-made figure. The lived knowledge: the critique of functioning labels as gatekeeping (Ollibean; the Autistic Self Advocacy Network; "Words That Wound," NeuroHub Community); the spiky profile; monotropism; and the difference-first frame of Helen Edgar's Why Difference Comes First (No. 11).

Held with care: refusing the severity line does not deny that some of us need far more support — functioning labels harm precisely because they gatekeep it. The just alternative is naming specific support needs: no rank, and all the care. The star stuff is Carl Sagan's, and the SN 1006 remnant on every Stimpunks page. The reading — that you are a fingerprint and a constellation, never a point on a line — is ours. You were ★stuff, and a whole spectrum, all along.
