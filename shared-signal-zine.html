The spectrum

One field
of signal

Tune a radio, warm your hands at a fire, catch a sunburn, sit for an X-ray — every time, you are using the same single thing. Radio waves, microwaves, infrared, visible light, ultraviolet, X-rays, gamma rays are not different substances. They are one electromagnetic field, vibrating at different speeds. Radio is only light too slow and long for our eyes; colour is just the signal our eyes happen to catch.

So there is no ladder of realness running from "in person" at the top down to "mere signal." There is one continuous spectrum, and we live our whole lives inside it. The voice on a call and the light off a face across a table are the same phenomenon, cut at different wavelengths.

One field. No separate signals — no more than there are separate airs.

Radio and starlight are the same thing, cut at different wavelengths.

Transmit · receive

The reciprocal
signal

Every signal is a two-sided act, the way every breath is. One body encodes — folds a voice, a word, a feeling onto a wave — and another decodes it back into meaning. Transmit and receive: the mirror of exhale and inhale.

And what rides the wave is more than information. Voice carries breath — the pace of a sentence, the pause before a hard word, the exhale under a laugh, sent unedited. A voice note is breath, timestamped and sent. You aren't only receiving data; you're receiving someone's nervous system, encoded in sound.

The physics doesn't care how far. The same law that carries your voice to a friend across the country carries a star's light across the galaxy. Encode, send, decode — it is how the universe has always passed itself along.

A voice note is breath — timestamped, and sent.
Shared Signal · Stimpunks 3
A term Helen coined

Shared-signal
space

Shared-air space is the room — proximity, breath, nervous systems settling into one another's rhythm. It needed a counterpart. Not a lesser one.

Helen Edgar named it: shared-signal space — connection carried on a signal instead of on the air of a room. A call, a message, a voice note, a synthesised voice: the rhythm still crosses, only now over distance.

Does it really carry? Measurably, yes — with an honest caveat. The tone and pace of a voice cue safety below awareness (Porges, 2011), and that cueing doesn't stop at the edge of a screen; two people's heart-rhythms even fall into sync across a video call, though weaker than in the same room (Streuber et al., 2026). Distance thins the channel. It does not empty it. A thinner channel is still a channel — and for many of us it is where regulation and honest connection come easiest.

Distance thins the channel. It does not empty it.

the rhythm still crosses
thinner, but real

Shared Signal · Stimpunks 4

thinking time · a record · fewer demands
the thought, let through

Text & AAC

The clearer
channel

For many Autistic people, the written word isn't a downgrade from speech — it's the channel that finally lets the thought through. Text takes away the real-time crush: no instant reply owed, no tone to decode on the fly, no face to read while hunting for words. A message can be drafted, reread, and fixed before it's sent, so what lands is closer to what was meant.

The largest survey of Autistic adults' own preferences found phone calls ranked last, again and again, and written channels near the top — for four steady reasons: thinking time, structure, fewer sensory demands, and a record to return to (Howard & Sedgewick, 2021).

The same is true, and truer, of AAC. Symbol boards, letterboards, text-to-speech run through the written channel, not the spoken one. For non-speaking and minimally speaking people, AAC isn't a workaround bolted onto "real" communication. It is the real communication.

Not a fallback from speech. For many of us, the version that finally lets the thought through.
Shared Signal · Stimpunks 5
Anything but the phone

Listen to the
phone-averse

"Anything but the phone!" is such a common Autistic refrain that researchers made it their title (Howard & Sedgewick, 2021). The live call asks for everything at once — instant response, tone read in real time, no record, no pause — and gives the least room to think.

Dread of that channel isn't rudeness or fragility, any more than the canary's alarm was weakness. It's information: a signal that this channel is costly, and that a better one exists. Offer the text option and watch who suddenly comes through clearly.

The fix isn't to toughen people up for phone calls. It's to stop treating one demanding channel as the toll for being reachable at all. Ask how someone would rather be reached — then reach them there.

Aversion to a demanding channel is information, not fragility.

the aversion is a signal
reach people where they come through

Shared Signal · Stimpunks 6

sideways & underground
lurking counts

Reachable, and for whom?

Build your own
watering hole

First the justice question, the twin of Shared Air's: reachable, and for whom? A signal life runs on data plans, devices, batteries, and platforms built by other people. When those cost too much or lock us out, "just message me" quietly excludes. Communication access is a commons, not a favour.

So we build. The Autistic Rhizome (Edgar, 2026) spreads sideways and underground — no ladder, no single correct path: a Discord alive at 2am, a thread reopened after three years as if no time had passed, a reply to a stranger that becomes a friendship. Online spaces work as watering holes — loosely held, come-and-go, where presence alone can sustain connection. Lurking counts. One reply after months counts.

Nobody handed us these rooms. We made them, and we keep them — infrastructure as mutual aid, in whatever medium lets us breathe.

Nota fallback for when the room isn't available — a mode of connection, not a workaround.
Notless true for being thinner — a thinner signal still carries a real rhythm.
Notranked beneath presence — signal is a form of presence.
Notalways spoken — written and AAC words carry understanding too, sometimes better.
Nota lesser community for having no address — a watering hole doesn't need a building.
Shared Signal · Stimpunks 7

light outlives the star
presence, sent ahead of the body

The oldest signal

The oldest
signal

Look up. Every star you see is a signal that outran its source — light that left so long ago that some of those stars are already gone. We wrote a whole zine about it: the dead stars still reach us. The reaching is real even when the one who sent it is out of reach.

That is the deepest thing a signal does: it lets connection cross a gap that touch cannot. The same physics that carries a dead star's light to your eye carries your voice to someone alive but far away. Presence, sent ahead of the body.

So no channel is the master channel — not voice, not video, not text, not breath. Connection is built between two people, in whatever medium lets both of them be understood (Milton, 2012). Shared air, shared signal: two real ways to hold each other's rhythm. Love you down to your star stuff — across any distance.

Signal lets connection cross a gap that touch cannot.

Shared Signal is the twenty-fifth zine in the Stimpunks series, and a companion to Shared Air (No. 24): breath and signal, two ways of holding each other's rhythm.

Shared-signal space is a term coined by Helen Edgar, as the counterpart to shared-air space: connection carried through signal rather than proximity, with no channel ranked master. This zine grows from a draft by Helen Edgar and Ryan Boren. The thinking and evidence: Helen Edgar's Autistic Rhizome (2026), on sideways, underground community and online watering holes; Howard & Sedgewick (2021), "Anything but the phone!", on Autistic communication-mode preferences; Stephen Porges (2011) on neuroception and vocal prosody; Streuber et al. (2026) on physiological synchrony across face-to-face, video, and VR; Damian Milton (2012), the double empathy problem; and Deleuze & Guattari (1987) on the rhizome. The physics: the electromagnetic spectrum as one continuous field — radio and light the same phenomenon at different wavelengths — and the light-travel time that lets a dead star still reach us.
