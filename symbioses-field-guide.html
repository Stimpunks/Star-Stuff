A Field Guide to
Symbioses

Mutual aid, written into biology. Fungi feeding trees, bacteria making soil, a fish that cleans the teeth of a predator that could swallow it. Look closely and the living world runs on partnership. Interdependence is the rule, not the exception — solidarity, not charity.

L★S
How to use this guide Each entry names a partnership, its two partners, what each one gives and gets — a reciprocity loop — and full field notes. Click any entry to open its notes.
Interdependence is the rule Kropotkin showed mutual aid is a factor of evolution (1902); Margulis, that life "took over the globe not by combat, but by networking" (Microcosmos, 1986). This isn't "nature is nice" — parasites are real too. It's that nothing living stands alone.
Solidarity, not charity What biology has always done, disability-justice communities name and practice. Independence is a myth (Mingus); mutual aid is collective coordination to meet each other's needs (Spade); self-care is birthed through community care (Talila A. Lewis).
Not: Not charity — solidarity. Not independence — interdependence; the self-made anything is a myth. Not "nature is nice" — the point is that no one survives alone; mutual aid is the part we choose. Not competition as life's engine — cooperation is at least as fundamental. Not leadership — competency networks; coordination over management.
Replace the language of management Language frames thought, so we swap the vocabulary of competition for the vocabulary of living systems (autcollab.): Symbiosis over competition Competency networks over leadership Coordination over management Values over value · creativity over best practices Human scale over endless growth
Star stuff Every partner in this guide is built from atoms forged in the same dying stars, shared out across all life. Cooperation runs all the way down — from the reef to the root nodule to the cell. It is time to celebrate our interdependence.

Not every symbiosis is gentle — some tilt toward exploitation, some run one way (the ghost pipe only takes), and "natural" was never a synonym for "good." The point isn't that biology is kind; it's that nothing living stands alone. Independence is the myth; interdependence is the fact; mutual aid is the kind we choose to build. Companion pieces: You Were Never One Thing (the collective inside a single cell), Underground (the mycelial worldview), No One Is the Leader (competency networks over command), and Helen Edgar's Monotropa uniflora and Monotropism (the ghost pipe, monotropism, and a tribute to Dinah Murray). We are caught in an inescapable network of mutuality (Martin Luther King Jr., Letter from Birmingham Jail, 1963).
