How knowledge grows

The garden,
not the stream

Most of the web is a social stream: a feed of dated posts, sorted by an algorithm, urgent for a day and then washed away. A post starts decaying the moment it's published. You don't return to it; you scroll past it.

A garden is the older idea. Mike Caulfield drew the line: the garden is the web as space, an arrangement you can walk. It's evergreen — you keep tending and refining the notes — and richly cross-linked, so that, as Maggie Appleton puts it, every walk creates new paths. You add things not in sequence but so they can form future, unpredicted relationships.

The Star Stuff site is a garden. So is the whole knowledge garden it grew from. Not a feed to keep up with — a landscape to wander.

A stream tells you what's new. A garden lets you wander what's true.

Lived experience

Grown from the
ground up

An encyclopedia grows downward from the top: subjects, then categories, then articles, handed down by an authority. A garden grows the other way. Ours climbs up from the soil of lived experience — from what a body knows, to a word for it, to a pattern, a recipe, an environment, a system.

And it doesn't grow in straight lines. Its shape is a rhizome and a mycelial network (after Deleuze & Guattari): ideas spread sideways and underground, throwing up shoots where they connect, refusing the neat trunk-and-branches of the tree.

Knowledge grown up from the ground of real neurodivergent and disabled life — not lowered down onto it.

An encyclopedia is handed down from the top. A garden grows up from the ground.
The Garden and the Stars · Stimpunks 3
The cosmic turn

Star stuff,
knowing itself

Here is the reason it's a garden and not a vault. Carl Sagan said we are a way for the cosmos to know itself. A knowledge garden is that sentence built into a thing you can walk through — and the method it uses is the cosmos's own.

A star forges the elements in its core and then, dying, scatters them — a supernova is a seed-head bursting (the image on every Stimpunks page, SN 1006, is literally liberated iron). That scattered stuff settles into life; life grows minds; minds grow gardens of meaning that go to seed and grow new minds. Nucleosynthesis, then the periodic table — the first seed catalogue — then us.

So the universe doesn't know itself as stored data. It knows itself as a garden — by growing, connecting, and seeding, over and over.

A knowledge garden is how star stuff comes to know itself.

scatter · grow · seed · again
a garden, not a vault

The commons

Held in common

A garden this open is a commons — like the old shared grazing lands and community forests, like open-source software: something people build, tend, and benefit from together. Ideas here are meant to circulate and evolve, designed collectively, made stronger by a diversity of minds.

The opposite of a garden is an enclosure: fence the field, paywall the knowledge, privatise the seed. We refuse that. The star stuff belongs to everyone because we're all made of it — and so does the knowledge grown from it. That's why every page is CC BY-SA, and every zine says print free, share freely.

Not owned. Tended, and given.

Notenclosed — a commons, not a fenced field.
Notextracted — knowledge here is tended and shared, not mined.
Nothanded down — grown up from lived experience.
Notfinished, and not anyone's to hoard — the seed is meant to travel.
The Garden and the Stars · Stimpunks 5
Blooms & dispersal

Flowers, and
going to seed

And this is where you are standing. If stimpunks.org is the garden, then starstuff.earth is where it flowers. These zines and field guides are the blooms — the same ideas from the beds, opened into colour so they can be seen from a distance.

A flower's whole purpose is to make seeds and give them away. So these are built to travel: open edition, print free, share freely isn't just a licence line — it is the seed dispersal. Print one, hand it on, tape it to a wall, replant it somewhere we'll never see. A question you bring to Ask is a seed too; every reader carries pollen between beds.

The bloom you're holding was grown to be given away.

A flower's only job is to give its seeds away.

The Garden and the Stars · Stimpunks 6

never finished, always tended

Tend, compost, regrow

The garden
is alive

A garden is never finished. It is tended and pruned, it goes to compost and comes back up; evergreen only means someone is always editing. Nothing here is carved in stone, because stone doesn't grow.

And you are not a visitor. In a garden the reader is soil and weather and pollinator — every walk wears a new path, every question plants something, every share scatters seed. The knowledge garden grows as people move through it.

The universe grows gardens to know itself — in every colour, key, and frequency of neurodiversity. And you, star stuff, are both a gardener and a bloom.

You are not visiting the garden. You are how it grows.

Reflection

Where in your life are you scrolling a stream when you could be tending a garden?

What knowledge have you grown from lived experience that no authority handed down?

What seed will you carry out of this garden and replant somewhere else?

The Garden and the Stars is the eighteenth zine in the Stimpunks series, and a self-portrait: it describes the very ground the others grew from. The knowledge garden and commons are Stimpunks' own; the flowering metaphor — the zines as blooms of that garden — is the Foundation's.

Digital gardening — evergreen notes, bi-directional links, seeds cultivated in public — is charted by Maggie Appleton and, canonically, by Mike Caulfield's "The Garden and the Stream" (2015); the deeper roots run to Vannevar Bush's memex ("As We May Think," 1945), Mark Bernstein's hypertext gardens, and Borges's garden of forking paths. The rhizome is Deleuze & Guattari, carried into the site's own Autistic Rhizome. The commons — shared grazing land, community forest, open source — is the old tradition Elinor Ostrom studied; everything here is Creative Commons BY-SA.

The cosmic turn is Carl Sagan's: we are made of star stuff, and a way for the cosmos to know itself. A supernova scatters the elements it forged — the SN 1006 remnant on every Stimpunks page is, in NASA's words, liberating iron — and stellar nucleosynthesis is the first seed catalogue. The reading — that the cosmos knows itself not as a vault but as a garden, and that widening it is how it grows — is ours. You were star stuff, and a bloom of the garden, all along.
