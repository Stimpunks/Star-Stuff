Phase transitions

Water doesn't get
gradually stiffer

Cool a glass of water and it does not slowly thicken toward solid. It stays liquid, liquid, liquid — and then, at a single temperature, it flips all at once into ice. Not a gradual stiffening; a sudden change of state.

It is the same H₂O on both sides — the very same molecules, only rearranged. Physicists call this a phase transition: a substance holding steady until a threshold in its conditions is crossed, then reorganising completely — solid, liquid, gas — with nothing added and nothing taken away.

Nothing about the water got better or worse. It crossed a line.

Nothing about the water changed. It crossed a line.

not a ramp — a cliff

The phase diagram

The conditions,
not the substance

Whether that water shows up as ice, liquid, or steam depends entirely on the conditions around it — temperature and pressure. Scientists chart it on a phase diagram: a map with a region for each state and lines where one becomes another.

Read it and the point lands quietly. Ice is not defective water; steam is not water trying harder. There is no best state and no failed state — only one substance, and the conditions it happens to be in.

So if you want a different state, you change the conditions, not the substance. We said this once about rooms and minds. Here it is again, about states.

Ice isn't broken water. It's water somewhere colder.

Fluctuating capacity

Why could you do
it yesterday?

Here is the question so many of us brace for: why could you do it yesterday and not today? However kindly it's meant, it lands like a charge of inconsistency — as if yesterday proved today is a choice.

The phase answer is simple. Capacity is a state, not a trait — and you were near a boundary. A little less sleep, one extra transition, a degree more noise, and the conditions tip. The same person crosses from managing to not managing, the way the same water crosses from liquid to ice on one degree of difference.

Not unreliable. Not lazy. Not putting it on. The same you, in changed conditions.

You are not inconsistent. You were near a boundary.

same person, tipped by conditions
a state, not a trait

Thresholds

The hardest part
is the crossing

There's a reason the change itself is the hard part. Right at a phase boundary a substance turns turbulent and hypersensitive — tiny nudges cause wild swings, because it's poised between two states at once.

That is threshold anxiety exactly: the dread that arrives before the crossing, the doorway harder than the room, the first day worse than the term. For a monotropic mind a transition means tearing attention out of one deep channel and re-forming it in another — and done suddenly, or piled up, it can pull the mind past what it can hold.

So the fix isn't a sturdier person. It's a better crossing — built with warning, with a way back, with time. Broken thresholds, not broken people.

Broken thresholds, not broken people.
The Same Water · Stimpunks 5
Weather-bodies

Held past the point,
slow to return

Two more facts, because they name things that get misread. Water can be supercooled — held below freezing and still liquid — until one small disturbance crystallises the whole glass in an instant. That is the meltdown after a day that "went fine": not one trivial last straw, but a system kept past its limit, tipping all at once.

And near a transition, systems show critical slowing down — they take far longer to settle after any knock. It isn't weakness that recovery swells near your edge; it's how systems behave there. Rest and regulation are the work, not a failure to do it.

Cynthia Kim calls this fluid adaptation: nothing has been fixed or broken; we hold a pattern, life changes, we lose the handle and find a new one — re-adapting across a lifetime, not regressing. Helen Edgar names us weather-bodies, always sensing and adjusting, and asks the better question: not how to make people more resilient to change, but how to make the climate more liveable.

Notbroken — the same substance, in colder conditions.
Notregressing — re-adapting to a changed climate.
Nota resilience problem — a climate problem, and climates can be redesigned.

The oldest change

The same water

This is the oldest move there is. The early universe didn't cool smoothly; it changed state — dropping past threshold after threshold, its forces freezing out of a single hot symmetry the way ice forms out of water. A cloud of gas drifts unchanged for ages, then crosses a threshold of density and collapses, all at once, into a star.

The same star stuff is plasma, gas, dust, rock, ocean, ice — and us. None of those is the broken version of the others. And the water in you is partly older than the Sun, carried in from the cold of space: it has been vapour and ice and rain and ocean, the same substance every time.

So when your state shifts with your conditions, you aren't failing. You're doing the most ancient thing matter knows how to do.

You were never broken. You were only ever water, meeting your conditions.

Reflection

What conditions were different on the day you "could do it" and the day you couldn't?

Where have you been supercooled — holding it together right up until one small thing?

What would change if the climate around you, not your resilience, were the thing to fix?

The Same Water is the twentieth zine in the Stimpunks series. It reads fluctuating capacity through the physics of phase transitions, and it extends Minor Worlds (No. 16): change the conditions, not the substance.

The science: phase transitions and the phase diagram; latent heat; supercooling and metastability; critical phenomena and critical slowing down; the symmetry-breaking transitions of the cooling early universe; the density threshold (Jeans instability) that collapses gas into stars; and the evidence that much of Earth's water predates the Sun. The lived knowledge is ours and our kin's: Cynthia Kim on autistic regression and fluid adaptation (Musings of an Aspie); Helen Edgar on weather-bodies and autistic weathering (More Realms); and Stimpunks' own threshold anxiety, with the monotropic split (Tanya Adkin & David Gray-Hammond), autistic inertia, and monotropism (Murray, Lesser & Lawson).

If meltdown, shutdown, or burnout are close to you right now, be gentle with yourself: rest and regulation are the work, and support is worth reaching for. The star stuff is Carl Sagan's, and the SN 1006 remnant on every Stimpunks page. The reading — that no state is the broken one, and the honest answer to a boundary is to change the conditions — is ours. You were ★stuff, meeting your conditions, all along.
