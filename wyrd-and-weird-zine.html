Before the insult

The word before
the insult

Long before "weird" meant strange, it meant becoming.

In Old English, wyrd comes from weorþan — "to happen, to occur, to become" (Stone, 2022). It wasn't a synonym for doom, and it wasn't a script waiting to be read out. It was the word for the process by which anything at all comes into being. A person's wyrd wasn't what had been written about them. It was what they were still becoming.

The same root gives us a second thread worth holding onto: Old English wyrd is twined with the ancestors of modern "worth" (Stone, 2022). Becoming and worth, tangled in the same word, from the start.

"Weird" isn't the opposite of normal. It's the oldest word we have for becoming.

becoming & worth, from the start
tangled in one word

The well beneath the tree

Worth,
not written

Norse tradition keeps fate's three keepers at a well beneath the world tree, Yggdrasill — Urð, Verðandi, and Skuld. Popular retellings gloss them as past, present, and future. That's tidy, and it isn't quite right (Stone, 2022).

Verðandi comes from verða, "to become." Skuld means "debt" or "tax." Urð may share a root with worth, or with a word meaning "to turn." Read plainly, together they describe not a plot but an account: what has come to be, what is happening now, and what is owed simply for having lived (Stone, 2022).

A life's wyrd was never a plot laid down before you were born. It was the worth a life comes to have, built thread by thread in the living of it. — reading Stone (2022), pp. 36–37

Weaving, not writing

The web,
not the line

Fate's keepers — the nornir, the Moirai, the Weird Sisters — are pictured again and again as spinners and weavers, working at a loom whose cloth is called, in one Old English poem, "the web of man" (Stone, 2022).

Notice the shape of that image. Fate was never drawn as a single straight line running from birth to death. It was drawn as cloth — many threads, crossing, holding each other's weight, none of them load-bearing alone.

A single thread can be cut. A web asks a different question: whose hands are helping hold it up?

A line has one direction. A web holds weight from every side.

cloth, not a single strand
many hands, many threads

How the word survived

Weird Sisters,
reclaimed

Wyrd stayed alive in English through women who unsettled people. Medieval, mostly Christian, writers kept the word for fate but let the goddess behind it quietly fade (Stone, 2022). By Shakespeare, they were the three Weird Sisters of Macbeth, drawn from Holinshed's Chronicles (1587) — feared, consulted, impossible to write out of the story.

A small honest note: Shakespeare's First Folio actually prints "weyward sisters." "Weird" is Holinshed's own word for them, fixed in place by later editors — which, if anything, makes the thread back to wyrd even more direct.

Centuries on, "weird" drifted again — from fate-word, to uncanny, to a casual insult for anything too strange to sit comfortably in the room. Autistic and disabled communities didn't start that drift. But plenty of us have chosen to run it backward: to pick "weird" up again as a name, not a verdict.

That's not just cheek. It's etymologically honest. We're not turning a slur into a compliment — we're giving an old, powerful word its meaning back.

Held with care

What wyrd
is not

Reclaiming a word means being clear about what we are not saying with it.

Nota script written before you arrived, with no part in it for you to weave.
Notdoom. Skuld's debt is the cost of having lived — not a punishment for how you lived it.
Nota synonym for wrong, broken, too much, or not enough.
Notsomething done to you alone. A web has many hands on it.

a web, not a single strand
whose hands help hold it?

The root, again

Becoming,
together

Weorþan — the root under wyrd — never meant "to be." It meant "to become." The self it describes isn't a fixed thing waiting at the end of a life; it's a self still happening, still owed, still worth building (Stone, 2022).

That's a strange kind of comfort, if you've ever been told your difference makes you incomplete, or unfinished, or not-yet-normal. Under the old word, nobody is finished. Becoming was never a flaw in the design. It was the design.

Nobody is finished. Becoming was never the flaw. It was the design.
